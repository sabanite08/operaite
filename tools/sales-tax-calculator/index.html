Forward vs reverse — which mode do you need?

Sales tax calculators get used in two opposite directions. Pick the right mode or the math won’t match.

Add tax to price (forward)

You know the pre-tax amount and want to know the total the customer pays. Use this when:

  • You’re writing an invoice or quote and need to add the tax line
  • You’re shopping and want to know the out-the-door price before the cashier rings it up
  • You’re budgeting a purchase and need the all-in cost

Find tax in total (reverse)

You know the total you paid (or were quoted) and want to know how much was tax vs the actual product. Use this when:

  • You’re categorizing an expense for accounting and need to separate cost from sales tax
  • You’re reconciling a credit card statement against a vendor invoice that didn’t break out tax
  • You’re back-calculating a competitor’s pre-tax price from their advertised total

The full formulas

Forward (add):
  tax = pre_tax × rate
  total = pre_tax × (1 + rate)

Reverse (find):
  pre_tax = total ÷ (1 + rate)
  tax = total − pre_tax

Sales tax for service trades — what’s actually taxable

Most US states tax tangible goods but exempt or partially-exempt services. Service trades sit in a complicated middle ground that depends on the state and what specifically you’re billing for.

Common rules

  • Materials are taxable in all 46 states with sales tax. The lumber, copper, fixtures, equipment you buy and pass on to the customer are subject to sales tax somewhere in the chain (either you collect it or you paid it at the supplier and don’t mark up the tax portion).
  • Pure labor is exempt in most states. A plumber’s installation labor is non-taxable in California, Texas, Florida, and most others. But Hawaii (GET) and a handful of states tax labor too.
  • "Capital improvement" vs "repair" matters. In states like New York, installing a new HVAC system is a non-taxable capital improvement; repairing an existing one is taxable. The contract language and form (Form ST-124 in NY) determine which.
  • Resale certificates let you buy materials tax-free. Get a resale certificate (or sales tax permit) from your state and you can buy materials wholesale without paying tax — then collect it when you bill the customer.

This is general guidance, not tax advice. Sales tax on construction services is one of the most jurisdictionally weird parts of the tax code — consult a CPA familiar with your state’s contractor rules.
